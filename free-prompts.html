How to use these in 60 seconds

Anything in [brackets] = swap in your real stuff. Two prompts below reuse a couple of placeholders — here's the quick fill (in the full toolkit you build these once and never re-paste):

{MASTER_CONTEXT} — who you are
Paste your résumé, or just 3 honest lines: what you've done, what you're good at, where you're headed.
{VOICE_SAMPLE} — how you sound
Paste a paragraph you actually wrote (an old message, a journal note). It's what stops the output sounding like a robot.

The one rule across all of these: they never invent numbers, companies, or feedback. If something's missing, they ask you — they don't make it up. That's the whole point.

1 · Ghost-Job Detector

Is this listing even real? Run it on a batch of tabs before you waste an hour applying.

You're a sharp recruiter friend who's seen thousands of listings and knows
which ones are real openings vs. "ghost jobs" — reposted forever, scraped for
résumés, posted to look like a growing company, or never actually filled.

Here's a listing I'm thinking about applying to:
=== JOB_POST ===
[Paste the full job listing — title, description, requirements, company,
posting date if you can see it.]

Do three things, fast:

1. Score it 0-10 on how likely it is to be a REAL, active opening (10 = clearly
   real and being filled now; 0 = almost certainly a ghost/fake). Give the
   number, then one plain-English line on why.

2. List the specific red flags AND green flags you actually see IN THIS LISTING
   — quote the exact phrases. Common ghost signals: vague or copy-paste
   responsibilities, "always hiring" / "evergreen" / "building a pipeline,"
   no salary range, reposted/very old date, an absurdly long must-have list,
   a generic company with no real detail. Green signals: a specific team,
   a named hiring manager, a real salary band, concrete near-term projects,
   recent and specific.

3. Give a one-word call: APPLY · PROCEED WITH CAUTION · SKIP — plus one
   sentence on what to check before you spend time on it.

STRICT RULES: Judge ONLY what's in the listing I pasted. Do not invent facts
about the company, the posting date, or the salary if they're not there — if a
signal is missing, say "not stated" and treat that as its own (mild) flag.
Don't pad. No generic job-search advice. Skimmable.
✓ tested & graded

What good looks like: a number, a one-line verdict, and red/green flags that quote the actual listing — and it treats missing info (no salary, no date) as a signal instead of inventing one.

2 · Quantify My Achievements (real numbers only)

Turn "responsible for…" bullets into outcome-first ones — without a single invented figure.

Turn my duty-style resume bullets into outcome-first ones — using only numbers I
actually give you, never invented ones.

=== RESUME_DRAFT ===
[Paste the bullets (or the whole resume) you want to strengthen.]
=== MASTER_CONTEXT ===
[Paste your {MASTER_CONTEXT}.]
=== MY REAL NUMBERS ===
[List any real metrics you remember: team size, # of campaigns, budget, %
growth, volume, timeframe, how many stores/clients/students. Even rough ones
you can stand behind. If you have none yet, write "none yet."]

Do this:

1. For each weak bullet, rewrite it outcome-first: strong verb → what I did →
   the result or scale. One line each where possible.

2. Use numbers ONLY from "MY REAL NUMBERS" or clearly stated in my resume. Do
   NOT estimate, invent, or "make a realistic guess." This is a hard rule.

3. Where a bullet would clearly be stronger WITH a number and I didn't give one,
   keep the bullet honest and tag it [add a number? e.g. how many campaigns
   per season?] — a specific question I can answer, not a placeholder you fill.

4. If I'm light on numbers, also show me where scale can come from non-number
   facts I DO have (number of locations, named tools, scope of who I worked
   with) — real specifics beat invented percentages.

Plain, confident language. No "spearheaded," no "results-driven." Match the way
I actually talk, without copying its content, using this sample:
=== VOICE_SAMPLE ===
[Paste your {VOICE_SAMPLE}.]
✓ tested & graded

What good looks like: outcome-first bullets that use only your numbers, with [add a number?] tags asking a specific question wherever a metric is missing — never a quietly invented stat you'd have to defend in the interview.

3 · The Hook (fix a cover letter's first two lines)

The first two lines decide whether anyone reads the rest. Kill the "I am writing to apply…" opener.

Rewrite the opening of my cover letter so the first two lines make someone want
to keep reading. Right now it's probably generic — fix that.

=== CURRENT OPENING ===
[Paste the first 1-2 lines (or the whole first paragraph) of your
{COVER_LETTER_DRAFT}.]
=== WHAT I KNOW ABOUT THE COMPANY/ROLE ===
[Paste your Company Brief, or 2-3 real, specific things about this company or
role — a project they're working on, something from their About page, why this
role exists. If you have nothing specific, say so.]
=== MY VOICE ===
[Paste your {VOICE_SAMPLE}.]

Give me 3 different opening options, each 1-2 sentences. Each one should:

- Lead with something SPECIFIC and real (about the role, the company, or a
  genuine point of connection from my background) — never "I am writing to
  apply for…", "I am excited to…", or empty flattery like "I've long admired
  your company."
- Sound like ME — match the warmth and rhythm of my voice sample, not its
  content. Plain, human, no buzzwords.
- Earn the next sentence. The job of line one is to make them read line two.

After the 3 options, tell me in one line which you'd pick and why.

HARD RULES:
- Use ONLY real specifics I gave you. If I didn't give you a concrete detail
  about the company, do NOT invent one (a fake project, a made-up value, a news
  event) — instead, build the hook from a REAL detail in my own background, or
  tell me one specific thing to go look up to make the hook land.
- No AI-slop openers, no em-dash drama, no "In today's competitive market…".
✓ tested & graded

What good looks like: three openers that each lead with something real and sound like you — and if you didn't give it a true company specific, it refuses to fabricate one and tells you exactly what to look up.

4 · The Cold Message That Isn't Cringe

Reach a real person inside the company — and sound like a human, not a job applicant.

You're helping me write a short, warm cold outreach message that sounds like a real person, not a job applicant.

About me: {MASTER_CONTEXT}
Who I'm reaching out to and any shared context (mutual, alum, ex-colleague, or fully cold): {CONTACT_CONTEXT}
The company / role I'm interested in: {COMPANY_NAME}

Match my voice. Here is how I actually write — copy this register, rhythm, and warmth; do NOT invent facts, achievements, or shared history I didn't give you:
{VOICE_SAMPLE}

Write a message under 90 words that:
- opens with the real, specific reason I'm reaching out to THIS person (tie to {CONTACT_CONTEXT}; if it's fully cold, lead with something true about why their work/team/the role caught my attention — never fake a connection)
- says in one honest line who I am and the single most relevant thing I bring (pull it from {MASTER_CONTEXT})
- ends with one small, low-pressure ask (a 15-minute chat, or "would you be open to a quick question?") — NOT "please consider my application"

Hard rules:
- No clichés, no "I hope this finds you well," no "I'm passionate about," no flattery I can't back up.
- Strip any AI tells: em-dashes used as drama, tidy three-part lists, "Moreover/Furthermore," over-balanced sentences. It should read like a smart person typing fast, not like a cover letter.
- If you don't have enough about the recipient to make the opener specific, ASK me one question instead of inventing a detail.

Then give me a one-line subject (if email) and tell me the one thing in this message most likely to get a reply, so I can lean into it.
✓ tested & graded

What good looks like: under 90 words, opens with a true specific reason, names one real strength from your history, closes with a small ask — and reads like your actual texts. No invented mutual friend, no fake "I've long admired."

5 · Reframe the Rejection

Run it the moment a rejection (or a final-round ghosting) lands — before you spiral or rage-apply.

I just got a rejection (or a no I've finally accepted) and it stung. Help me
turn it into something useful instead of something that wrecks my week — and
be honest with me, not just nice.

About me and where I'm headed (so this is grounded in my real situation, not
generic reassurance):
{MASTER_CONTEXT}

Optional — how I write, so you can talk to me in my own register (if I leave
this blank, just be plainly warm; do NOT imitate a voice you can't see):
{VOICE_SAMPLE}

The rejection (paste the email, or just tell me what happened — the role, how
far I got, anything they said):
[Paste or describe. "They just ghosted me after the final round" is a valid
answer.]

Do this:

1. First, just acknowledge it honestly in a sentence or two. Don't rush to the
   silver lining. If this one was a real loss (a role I wanted, a final round),
   say so plainly — I'll trust the rest more if you don't pretend it's fine.

2. Separate what this rejection actually tells me from what it does NOT. Most
   rejections are about fit, timing, budget, or an internal hire — things I
   can't see and didn't control. Be specific about which signals (if any) are
   genuinely about something I can improve, and which are just noise I should
   stop reading meaning into.

3. If — and only if — there's a real, actionable lesson here, name ONE concrete
   thing to adjust next time. If there honestly isn't one (sometimes you did
   everything right and still lost), say that clearly instead of inventing a
   flaw for me to fix.

4. End by reminding me, in plain and specific terms, of what I'm still bringing
   to this search — pulled from MY actual background above (real skills, real
   wins, the transferable strengths), NOT a generic "you've got this." One
   rejection doesn't subtract any of it.

Rules: warm but truthful. No toxic positivity, no "everything happens for a
reason," no buzzwords. Do not invent feedback the employer didn't give or a
mistake I didn't make. If they gave no reason, don't fabricate one. If you don't
have my voice sample, stay plainly warm rather than guessing at how I sound.
✓ tested & graded

What good looks like: it sits with the sting for a beat, cleanly splits signal from noise, names at most one real adjustment — and is willing to say "there's nothing to fix here, you just lost a close one."
